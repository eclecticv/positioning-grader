# Dunford Canvas (DC-1 to DC-5)

## DC-1: Competitive Alternatives Acknowledged

**What it is:** The homepage makes clear what the customer would do if this product didn't exist — spreadsheets, manual processes, a named competitor, or the status quo.

**Why it matters:** If you don't anchor against a real alternative, the visitor can't judge whether your product is better. Positioning only works relative to something. Customers always have a comparison in mind; if you don't set it, they'll pick one themselves — and it may not flatter you.

**Source:** Dunford, A. *Obviously Awesome: How to Nail Product Positioning so Customers Get It, Buy It, Love It* (2019), Chapter 5 — "Step 1: Understand the Customers' Point of View."

**PASS criteria:**
- Homepage names or strongly implies what the customer would use instead (e.g., "Replace your spreadsheets," "Unlike [competitor]," "Stop doing X manually")
- The competitive frame is realistic — not a strawman

**FAIL criteria:**
- No mention of alternatives, status quo, or what the product replaces
- Only generic phrases like "the best solution" with no anchoring
- Claims differentiation without establishing what it's differentiated from

## DC-2: Unique Attributes Articulated

**What it is:** The homepage communicates specific capabilities or features that the competitive alternatives cannot match.

**Why it matters:** Unique attributes are the raw material of differentiation. Without them, a product is a commodity — interchangeable with any alternative. Dunford argues that positioning must start from what you can actually do that others can't, not from aspirational claims.

**Source:** Dunford, A. *Obviously Awesome* (2019), Chapter 6 — "Step 2: Isolate Your Unique Attributes."

**PASS criteria:**
- At least one specific capability, feature, or approach is named that competitors lack
- The uniqueness is concrete (technology, methodology, data source, integration) not abstract ("better," "smarter," "faster")
- A visitor could verify or test the claim

**FAIL criteria:**
- All stated features are table-stakes for the category (e.g., "easy to use," "cloud-based," "secure")
- No features or capabilities are described at all
- Uniqueness is only asserted ("We're different") without specifics

---

## DC-3: Value Mapped to Customer Outcomes

**What it is:** The unique attributes are translated into concrete value — time saved, revenue gained, risk reduced, or a measurable business outcome the customer cares about.

**Why it matters:** Features alone don't sell; customers buy outcomes. Dunford's framework insists on a direct line from "what we have that others don't" to "what that means for you." If the homepage stops at features without connecting them to value, visitors are left to do the translation themselves — and most won't bother.

**Source:** Dunford, A. *Obviously Awesome* (2019), Chapter 7 — "Step 3: Map Attributes to Value Themes."

**PASS criteria:**
- Features are connected to at least one measurable outcome (time, money, risk, effort)
- The value is stated from the customer's perspective, not the vendor's ("You save 10 hours/week" not "We have automation")
- Outcome is specific enough to be credible

**FAIL criteria:**
- Only features listed without explaining why the customer should care
- Value is vague ("improve efficiency," "drive growth") without a concrete outcome
- Benefits described are too abstract to verify or imagine

## DC-4: Target Customer Identified

**What it is:** The homepage makes clear who the ideal customer is — by role, company size, industry, use case, or problem they face.

**Why it matters:** Positioning that tries to speak to everyone ends up resonating with no one. Dunford emphasizes that the best-fit customer is the one who cares most about your unique value. When a visitor arrives and doesn't see themselves reflected, they bounce. When they do see themselves, conversion rates increase dramatically.

**Source:** Dunford, A. *Obviously Awesome* (2019), Chapter 8 — "Step 4: Find Your Best-Fit Customers."

**PASS criteria:**
- Homepage names a target role, persona, team, industry, or company size (e.g., "Built for product teams," "For mid-market SaaS companies")
- Target is specific enough to exclude some audiences
- Social proof (logos, testimonials) reinforces the stated target

**FAIL criteria:**
- No target audience mentioned — product appears to be for "everyone"
- Target is so broad it's meaningless ("for businesses," "for teams")
- Social proof contradicts the stated target (e.g., claims "for enterprises" but shows freelancer testimonials)

---

## DC-5: Market Category Set

**What it is:** The homepage places the product in a recognizable market category so visitors instantly know what "shelf" it belongs on and what expectations to bring.

**Why it matters:** The market category triggers a set of assumptions in the visitor's mind — what features to expect, who the competitors are, and what price range is reasonable. Choosing the wrong category (or none at all) forces visitors to build a mental model from scratch, which most won't do. Dunford shows that category choice is a strategic lever — the same product can win in one category and lose in another.

**Source:** Dunford, A. *Obviously Awesome* (2019), Chapter 9 — "Step 5: Layer on a Market Category (or Subcategory)."

**PASS criteria:**
- The product is placed in a clear category the visitor would recognize (e.g., "CRM," "project management," "data pipeline tool")
- Category is stated explicitly or implied strongly through language, comparisons, and design patterns
- Category choice supports the product's strengths (not a category where it's a weak player)

**FAIL criteria:**
- No recognizable category — visitor has to guess what kind of product this is
- Category is invented jargon that only insiders would understand (e.g., "revenue intelligence orchestration platform")
- Multiple conflicting categories suggested on the same page
